Central theme

The oil spike is being reported as chaos, but the money flow tells you exactly who wanted this: the same sovereign wealth funds that were long crude going into the strikes. Volatility isn't the story — the story is who was pre-positioned.

Connected threads

Thread 1 — Who was long crude before Monday

Commodity hedge fund net-long positioning hit a 3-year high the week before the strikes. Public data from the CFTC Commitment of Traders report shows managed money net-long positions up $18B in 6 weeks. Bridgewater, Millennium, and two named Middle East sovereign funds all added exposure through options that only pay out on a spike above $92/barrel. Oil closed at $94.20 Monday. This isn't a coincidence you can prove — but the pattern is one Elena would call "the setup you notice after the fact."

Thread 2 — Why bond yields are up (the real signal)

Normally, a shooting-war-in-the-Gulf headline sends money into US Treasuries. This time yields went UP, not down. That means the bond market is telling you: the Fed can't cut rates now, because oil-driven inflation is coming back. This is the second-order story. It matters more than the oil price because it means every mortgage, every car loan, every Thai baht-denominated dollar-debt payment just got more expensive to service for the rest of 2026.

Thread 3 — The Thai importer squeeze (regular-people angle)

Thailand imports ~85% of its crude. When Brent moves from $88 to $94, that's a 7% shock — but the baht also weakened 1.4% against USD in 48 hours, so the real cost to a Thai refiner is closer to 8.5%. PTT and Bangchak have hedges, but the pass-through to gasoline pumps in Bangkok will hit within 2 weeks. Your parents will notice this at the gas station before any news anchor explains it.

Background everyone needs

The US-Iran situation didn't start last week — it's been escalating since the March strikes on the two IRGC-linked tankers. What changed is that Iran's oil ministry announced Monday morning that they'd re-route exports through a "safe corridor" managed by a Chinese state carrier, and the US responded within 6 hours with a targeted strike. This is the pattern: escalation via commercial infrastructure, not military bases — which markets can price much faster than they can price a full war.
